5 Reasons Why You Should Hire A Musical Programmer
bloom.510

As it turns out, musicality seems to be a powerful predictor of coding success. "The greatest scientists are artists as well," the genius Albert Einstein once said, but why are musicians uniquely suited to be software developers?...[1]


A unique combination of creative and analytical skills

...similar talents or brain areas may underlie both musical composition and computer programming... [I]n many labs doing electronic media around Chicago, the programmers are often musicians who began programming computers late in life, and had none of the normally associated background education (i.e. mathematics)... music and programming both use the same part of the brain - the part responsible for "visual symbolic manipulation".[2]

Firm understanding of High Level Design.

Just like music, programming has form, structure, and language.
[description of aforementioned building blocks applied to music and relevance to coding]

musicians will adapt well to programming due to their visual abilities—or “spatial/temporal” skills, in the parlance of physics professor Gordon L. Shaw, who co-discovered the “Mozart effect,” which highlighted the positive effects classical music has on reasoning. “To construct a good program, you want to be able to see the consequences in your head, not just do line by line of the code,” Shaw says. “You have to be able to totally visualize it.”

Familiarity with combinatorics and modularity

“Music, especially on the piano, forces your brain to think and your body to operate in multiple layers. This translates effectively into coding, especially as we get into building more in-depth web applications. You have to be able to know how to organize a large amount of complexity in the concepts you’re learning, and to triage and prioritize an overwhelming amount of information at one time.”

Let's get analytical for a second. What if I divided an octave evenly into 9 parts?
[C, C#, D, D#, E, F, F#, G, G#, A, A#, B] --> [C, C# D, E, F, F#, G#, A, A#]

I could arrange these 9 notes into various configurations:

  1. Three augmented triads a semitone apart,
  2. Two augmented scales a semitone apart,
  3. Three double harmonic minor scales stratified in major thirds.,
  4. Any combination of major, minor, diminished, augmented triads from each of those scales.
Now let's get creative! Bizarre yet true, this harmonic system exhibits radial symmetry emanating from the tritone of each double harmonic minor scale. I could then systematically create families of extended triads by symmetrically adjusting notes in the triad. I could even go as far to create 4-note voicings. Now posessing multiple techniques for navigating this geometric structure, one can compose mandallic music with this harmonic shape in mind.
